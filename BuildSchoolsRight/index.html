We want you to use our quick and easy email tool (below) to tell BC’s government leaders that it’s time to update the school design policy.

Here’s why: Everyone knows that BC’s century-old schools are seismically unsafe and often contain other health hazards, like asbestos and lead. Usually the best way to address these issues is to build a brand new replacement school. However, communities overjoyed to welcome a safer school are dismayed to find out that the new schools are on average 30% smaller than the schools they are replacing, and they lack important learning spaces.

This is due to the Ministry of Education’s policy (the “Area Standards”) that sets out how schools are to be built in BC and determines the amount of money that will be budgeted for construction of a school. The policy does not allocate space for art rooms, music rooms, adequate hallway space, washrooms, staff rooms, sufficient gymnasium space, and other necessities for successful learning. BC’s policy allows for less space per child than other provinces with similar policies. For more information, read PAN’s detailed description of the problems with the Area Standards policy.

Some examples of problematic new builds include General Gordon Elementary, where the new school is too small to accommodate the children in its catchment; Eric Hamber Secondary, where the proposed replacement school will be built without an auditorium/performing arts theatre and with less gym space; and Bayview Elementary, which will lose its kiln—a crucial part of its fine arts program—because there is no space for it in the proposed replacement school. While these are Vancouver schools, this is not a Vancouver-specific issue. The policy applies to new school construction throughout the province.

The Ministry of Education has responded to concerns about this policy by stating that it plans to review the policy but in the meantime districts have “flexibility” to design schools as long as they stay within budget. This is not satisfactory: How can an art room, music room, or auditorium be built if the policy that decides the budget does not allow for it to be included? What other necessary spaces, allowed under the policy, would have to be sacrificed instead?

We need to ensure that we are not only building safe schools, we are building the right schools for student success.
